03 · Audio Preprocessing & Manipulation

The "data cleaning" step of the audio world — shaping raw signal before any model or feature extractor sees it.

Overview

Raw recordings are messy: silence at the start and end, inconsistent loudness between files, background hiss, a rumble from a desk mic. Preprocessing is the set of standard steps that turn a messy raw recording into something consistent enough for a model — or a human labeler — to work with reliably. Get this step wrong and every downstream model inherits the problem.

Key concepts

Loudness normalization

Peak normalization

Scales a clip so its loudest single sample hits a target level (e.g. -1 dBFS). Simple, but two clips peak-normalized to the same level can still sound very differently loud overall.

Loudness normalization (RMS / LUFS)

Targets perceived loudness averaged over time, not just the single loudest sample. LUFS (Loudness Units Full Scale) is the broadcast/streaming standard — this is what you want for consistent training data.

Silence trimming & voice activity detection (VAD)

VAD classifies each short frame of audio as "speech" or "not speech." At its simplest, that's an energy threshold (frames quieter than X are silence); more robust VADs are small trained classifiers that hold up under background noise. Used to trim dead air from clip edges, and to split long recordings into speech segments before anything else runs on them.

Denoising & filtering

  • High-pass filter — removes low-frequency rumble and DC offset (desk vibration, mic handling noise, HVAC hum) while leaving speech frequencies intact.
  • Low-pass filter — removes high-frequency hiss and out-of-band noise.
  • Spectral / learned denoising — classic methods estimate a noise profile and subtract it in the frequency domain (spectral subtraction/gating); modern approaches use a small neural network trained to separate speech from noise directly.

Framing & windowing

Most processing (including the STFT from lesson 01) doesn't operate on the whole clip at once — it slides a short frame (e.g. 25 ms) across the audio with some overlap (a hop of e.g. 10 ms), applying a tapered window function (commonly Hann) to each frame before analyzing it. The taper avoids the abrupt edges a hard rectangular cut would introduce, which would otherwise smear energy across frequencies that were never actually present.

Each shaded band is one analysis window; windows overlap so no part of the signal is under-analyzed at the frame edges.

Augmentation

Deliberately distorting training audio to make models robust to conditions they'll meet in the real world:

  • Pitch shift — raise/lower pitch without changing duration
  • Time-stretch — change speaking speed without changing pitch
  • Noise injection — mix in background noise (café, traffic, static)
  • SpecAugment — mask random blocks of time or frequency directly on the spectrogram, forcing the model to not over-rely on any single band or moment

Order matters

Denoise before normalizing loudness, not after. Denoising changes a signal's energy (removing noise energy shifts the overall level), so if you normalize first, denoising just undoes your target loudness. Normalizing last is what makes the final output actually land on the loudness you asked for.

A little history

  • 1979 — Spectral subtractionSteven Boll's classic algorithm estimates a noise spectrum during silence and subtracts it from the signal — the ancestor of most classical denoising.
  • 1990s — Statistical VADEnergy- and statistical-model-based voice activity detectors become standard in telephony (e.g. codec silence suppression).
  • 2017 — RNNoiseA small recurrent network shows learned denoising can outperform classical spectral methods, running in real time on modest hardware.
  • 2019 — SpecAugmentGoogle shows that simple time/frequency masking on spectrograms, with no extra data, meaningfully improves ASR robustness — now a default in most speech training pipelines.

Evaluation

SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio) is the primary metric — the ratio of desired signal power to noise power, in decibels, comparing before/after denoising. For perceptual quality of speech enhancement specifically, PESQ and STOI are common standardized metrics that correlate better with human judgment than raw SNR.
