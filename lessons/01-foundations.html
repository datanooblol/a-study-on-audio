01 · Foundations of Digital Audio

How sound becomes numbers — and the vocabulary every later lesson assumes you already have.

Overview

A microphone captures a continuous, analog vibration in the air. A computer can only store discrete numbers. Everything in this curriculum — Whisper transcribing a meeting, a TTS model producing a voice, a diarization model figuring out who spoke when — starts from the same conversion: turning that continuous wave into an array of numbers, and often into a picture of frequency-over-time called a spectrogram. This lesson is the vocabulary for that conversion.

Key concepts

Sampling & sample rate

Sampling measures the amplitude of a sound wave at regular intervals. Sample rate is how many measurements are taken per second, in Hertz (Hz). A 16,000 Hz (16 kHz) recording has 16,000 numbers for every second of audio.

Nyquist–Shannon theorem

To faithfully represent a frequency, you must sample at at least twice that frequency. A sample rate of 16 kHz can represent frequencies up to 8 kHz (its Nyquist frequency). Human speech's important content sits mostly below 8 kHz, which is why 16 kHz is the default for speech models. Music and general audio need more headroom for high-frequency detail, which is why "CD quality" is 44.1 kHz.

The continuous wave (faint line) only ever exists in the recording as the sampled points (dots). Everything the computer knows about the sound is in those numbers.

Bit depth & quantization

Sample rate is how often you measure; bit depth is how precisely each measurement is stored. 16-bit audio stores each sample as one of 65,536 possible amplitude values; 24-bit gives ~16.7 million. More bits mean a larger dynamic range (quietest to loudest sound represented without distortion) — roughly 6 dB of dynamic range per bit.

Time domain vs. frequency domain

Waveform (time domain)

Amplitude vs. time. Good for seeing loudness, silence, and timing — but a vowel and a consonant can look like similar squiggles.

Spectrogram (frequency domain)

Frequency vs. time, with color/intensity showing energy. Good for seeing what sound is present — vowels, pitch, harmonics — which is what most audio ML models actually learn from.

A spectrogram: darker/brighter bands ("formants") track how a speech sound's dominant frequencies move over time. This is produced by the Fourier Transform, below.

The Fourier Transform & STFT

The Fourier Transform is the math that says: any complex waveform can be decomposed into a sum of simple sine waves at different frequencies, phases, and amplitudes. Feed it a chunk of audio, and it tells you "how much of each frequency is present" — that's a spectrum.

A single spectrum only describes one moment. The Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) slides a small window across the signal, computing a spectrum for each window position. Stack those spectra side by side over time and you get a spectrogram — the frequency-vs-time picture almost every audio model in this curriculum is either built from or trained to produce.

Choosing a format for your own work

When you go looking for real datasets or record your own, match the sample rate, bit depth, and format to the task rather than defaulting to "highest quality possible":

TaskTypical sample rateBit depthFormat

Rule of thumb

Match the rate a pretrained model was trained on (most speech models standardize on 16 kHz internally, even if you feed them something else). Extra resolution the model was never trained on is wasted storage, not free accuracy. Keep an archival master at the highest quality you captured, and derive task-specific copies from it — more on exactly how to do that safely in the next lesson.

A little history

  • 1928 / 1949 — Nyquist & ShannonHarry Nyquist and later Claude Shannon formalized the sampling theorem that still governs every digital recording made today.
  • 1960s–70s — Digital telephonyPhone networks standardized on 8 kHz sampling (later mu-law/A-law encoded) — just enough for intelligible speech over a narrow channel. That legacy is why some speech datasets and legacy telephony/IVR systems still use 8 kHz.
  • 1982 — Compact Disc44.1 kHz / 16-bit stereo PCM became the consumer "CD quality" standard, chosen to comfortably exceed the ~20 kHz upper limit of human hearing.
  • 2010s–today — ML-driven standardizationModern speech models (Whisper, wav2vec2, HuBERT) converged on 16 kHz mono as the working rate for speech — high enough for intelligibility, small enough to keep models and datasets fast to train on.
