09 · Text-to-Speech (TTS)

Turning text into a waveform — three stages that have been gradually merging into one model.

Overview

TTS is the first "generate audio" lesson: instead of extracting information from audio, we produce it. Classic TTS breaks into three fairly independent stages; the story of the field since 2016 has been each stage getting replaced by a neural network, and the stages themselves gradually merging into a single trained model.

Key concepts

The three-stage pipeline

  1. Text normalization — expand things that aren't read as written: "Dr." → "Doctor", "3/4" → "three quarters" (or "March fourth", depending on context) — plus grapheme-to-phoneme (G2P) conversion to resolve pronunciation.
  2. Acoustic model — predicts an intermediate representation (almost always a mel-spectrogram) from the phoneme sequence, modeling duration, pitch, and energy — the prosody of speech.
  3. Vocoder — converts that mel-spectrogram into an actual waveform. This is a genuinely hard inverse problem: computing a mel-spectrogram threw away phase information, so the vocoder has to plausibly reconstruct it.

Why quality historically lagged in each stage

Concatenative synthesis

Stitches together fragments of real recorded speech. Individual sounds are natural, but requires a huge recorded corpus and can sound discontinuous at the seams between fragments.

Parametric synthesis

Statistically generates spectral parameters (originally via HMMs) instead of storing audio. Small footprint and smooth, but characteristically "buzzy" or muffled — recognizably synthetic.

Neural models replaced both: neural acoustic models (Tacotron, FastSpeech) generate natural prosody directly, and neural vocoders (WaveNet, HiFi-GAN) generate waveforms detailed enough to sound convincingly human.

Where zero-shot systems diverge

Modern large-scale systems (Bark, VALL-E-style models) skip the explicit mel-spectrogram pipeline entirely. They treat speech generation as a language-modeling problem over discrete audio tokens (produced by a neural audio codec, e.g. Encodec), and can condition on a few seconds of reference audio to imitate a voice without any fine-tuning — an "in-context learning" approach to voice that leads directly into voice cloning (lesson 10).

A little history

  • 1930s–1980s — Formant & rule-based synthesisEarly synthesizers (starting with Bell Labs' Voder, 1939) generate speech from explicit rules about formant frequencies — intelligible but unmistakably robotic.
  • 1990s–2000s — Concatenative / unit-selection synthesisSystems stitch together fragments of real recorded speech, requiring huge voice corpora.
  • 2000s — Statistical parametric synthesisHMM-based systems generate spectral parameters statistically — smaller footprint, but characteristically buzzy-sounding.
  • 2016 — WaveNetDeepMind's autoregressive neural vocoder generates raw audio sample-by-sample, a massive quality leap — but far too slow for real-time use at first.
  • 2017–2018 — Tacotron / Tacotron 2Attention-based sequence-to-sequence neural acoustic models generate mel-spectrograms directly from text, paired with a neural vocoder.
  • 2019 — FastSpeechNon-autoregressive acoustic models with explicit duration prediction fix Tacotron's occasional word-skipping/repeating and run much faster.
  • 2020 — HiFi-GANA GAN-based vocoder reaches near-WaveNet quality while generating audio in one fast pass — becomes the new default vocoder.
  • 2021 — VITSCombines the acoustic model and vocoder into one end-to-end model trained jointly with adversarial training, removing the two-stage pipeline.
  • 2023+ — Zero-shot, token-based TTSBark and VALL-E-style models generate speech as discrete audio-codec tokens via a language model, enabling zero-shot voice adaptation from seconds of reference audio.

Evaluation

MOS (Mean Opinion Score) is the historical standard: human listeners rate naturalness on a 1–5 scale, averaged across raters and samples. It's the ground truth the field optimizes toward, despite being slow and expensive to collect (automatic MOS-prediction models exist as a faster proxy). Intelligibility is checked by transcribing the synthesized speech with an ASR model and computing WER against the original input text — a proxy for "can this actually be understood."
