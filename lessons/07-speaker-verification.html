07 · Speaker Verification & Identification

Voice biometrics — "is this voice who it claims to be," reusing diarization's embeddings for a different job.

Overview

Diarization (lesson 06) groups voices without knowing who they belong to. Verification and identification go the other way: given a known set of enrolled voices, decide whose voice a new sample belongs to — or whether it belongs to any of them. This is voice biometrics, and it reuses the exact same speaker-embedding technology as diarization, just applied to matching a person instead of clustering strangers.

Key concepts

Verification vs. identification

Verification (1:1)

"Is this you?" Given a claimed identity and a voice sample, compare against that one person's enrolled voiceprint and accept or reject. Used for voice login/2FA.

Identification (1:N)

"Whose voice is this?" Compare a sample against every enrolled voiceprint and return the best match — or, in the open-set case, "none of the above" if nothing matches well enough.

Enrollment

Before verification can happen, a person's voice must be enrolled: capturing one or more reference utterances and converting them into a stored embedding (a "voiceprint"), often averaged across several samples to reduce noise from any one recording.

Speaker embeddings

The same family of embeddings used in diarization, trained specifically to make same-speaker vectors close together and different-speaker vectors far apart:

  • d-vectors — taken from a bottleneck layer of a network trained to classify speakers
  • x-vectors — a time-delay neural network (TDNN) with statistics pooling over frames; the embedding that displaced d-vectors as the standard
  • ECAPA-TDNN — adds channel attention and multi-scale feature aggregation on top of the x-vector idea; the current default in most open-source toolkits

Matching is typically done with cosine similarity between two embeddings, or a learned scoring model like PLDA (Probabilistic Linear Discriminant Analysis) for better robustness to channel/recording-condition mismatch.

Anti-spoofing & liveness

Why this matters

A recorded replay of someone's voice — or, increasingly, a synthetic clone (lessons 09–10) — can fool a naive verification system. Production voice-biometric systems need dedicated anti-spoofing detectors trained to distinguish live human speech from replayed or synthetically generated audio. This arms race is exactly why voice cloning technology (lesson 10) raises real security concerns.

A little history

  • 1990s — GMM-UBMGaussian Mixture Model / Universal Background Model becomes the standard classical approach: model a speaker as a deviation from a generic "background" voice model.
  • 2007 — PLDA scoringPrince & Elder introduce PLDA as a more robust way to score similarity between speaker representations.
  • 2011 — i-vectorsDehak et al. introduce a compact, factor-analysis-based fixed-length representation that becomes dominant for years.
  • 2018 — x-vectorsSnyder et al. show deep, discriminatively trained TDNN embeddings outperform i-vectors, becoming the new standard.
  • 2020 — ECAPA-TDNNDesplanques et al. push x-vectors further with attention and multi-scale aggregation — today's default in open-source toolkits.
  • 2015–ongoing — ASVspoof challengesA recurring benchmark series drives anti-spoofing research as synthetic and cloned voices become more convincing.

Evaluation

EER (Equal Error Rate) is the standard metric: the point on the accept/reject threshold curve where the False Acceptance Rate (wrongly accepting an impostor) equals the False Rejection Rate (wrongly rejecting the real person). A single number summarizing the whole trade-off — lower is better. The full trade-off curve across all thresholds is called a DET curve.
