05 · Speech-to-Text (ASR)

From HMM-GMM pipelines to a single Transformer that just transcribes.

Overview

Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) converts spoken audio into text. It's one of the oldest AI problems (research goes back to the 1950s) and one of the most commercially important — captions, voice assistants, meeting transcription, dictation. The field's history is really the story of two separately-trained components (an acoustic model and a language model) gradually merging into one network trained end-to-end.

Key concepts

Acoustic model vs. language model

Classically, ASR split into two models glued together by a search algorithm:

Acoustic model

Maps audio features (lesson 04) to probabilities over sounds/phonemes — "what does this audio sound like it contains?"

Language model

Scores how probable a word sequence is in the target language — "is this a sentence someone would actually say?" Helps resolve acoustic ambiguity (e.g. "recognize speech" vs. "wreck a nice beach").

A decoding search (historically Viterbi/beam search over an HMM) combined both scores to find the most likely text. Modern end-to-end models still implicitly do both jobs — they just learn them jointly from paired audio-text data instead of training and combining two separate models.

CTC vs. sequence-to-sequence decoding

CTC (Connectionist Temporal Classification)

A loss function that lets a model output one prediction per audio frame — including a special "blank" symbol — without needing frame-by-frame text labels. Repeated symbols and blanks collapse into the final text. Fast, non-autoregressive, but assumes outputs are independent given the audio.

Attention-based seq2seq

Treats ASR like translation: an encoder processes the audio, a decoder generates text one token at a time, attending back over the audio as needed. Naturally models dependencies between output tokens, at the cost of slower, autoregressive decoding.

Where Whisper fits

Whisper is an attention-based encoder-decoder Transformer, notable less for architectural novelty and more for scale and data diversity: it was trained on 680,000 hours of weakly-supervised, multilingual, multitask audio scraped from the web. That scale gives it strong robustness to accents, background noise, and domain shift — problems earlier systems needed careful, task-specific engineering to handle.

Multilingual & noisy-audio challenges

  • Accent and dialect variation within a single "language"
  • Code-switching (speakers mixing languages mid-sentence)
  • Low-resource languages with little transcribed training data
  • Background noise, overlapping speakers, far-field/reverberant recordings
  • Domain-specific vocabulary (medical, legal, technical jargon)

A little history

  • 1950s–70s — Template matchingIsolated-word systems compare incoming audio to stored templates using dynamic time warping.
  • 1980s–2000s — HMM-GMM eraHidden Markov Models with Gaussian Mixture acoustic models, combined with n-gram language models, become the dominant paradigm for decades of research and commercial systems.
  • ~2012 — Deep acoustic modelsDeep neural networks replace GMMs as the acoustic model in hybrid DNN-HMM systems — one of deep learning's first major wins outside computer vision.
  • 2014–2015 — End-to-end arrivesCTC-based systems (e.g. Baidu's Deep Speech) and attention-based seq2seq models (Listen, Attend and Spell) show a single network can replace the whole classical pipeline.
  • 2020 — Self-supervised pretrainingwav2vec2 shows pretraining on unlabeled audio then fine-tuning on small labeled sets can rival fully-supervised systems, a big win for low-resource languages.
  • 2022 — WhisperOpenAI releases a single multilingual, multitask Transformer trained on massive weakly-labeled web audio, setting a new practical bar for general-purpose, robust ASR.

Evaluation

WER (Word Error Rate) is the standard metric:

WER = (Substitutions + Deletions + Insertions) / Total words in reference

Lower is better; 0% is a perfect transcript. For languages without clear word boundaries (e.g. Chinese, Japanese), CER (Character Error Rate) is used instead, computed the same way at the character level.
