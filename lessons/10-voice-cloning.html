10 · Voice Cloning

Adapting TTS to speak in a specific person's voice — and the technology it borrows straight from speaker verification.

Overview

Voice cloning takes the TTS pipeline from lesson 09 and conditions it on a specific person's voice instead of a generic one, using anywhere from a few minutes down to a few seconds of their reference audio. The core trick is a direct callback to lesson 07: the same kind of speaker embedding used to verify a voice can be reused to condition a generative model on that voice instead.

Key concepts

Embedding conditioning vs. fine-tuning

Speaker-embedding conditioning (zero/few-shot)

Extract a speaker embedding from a short reference clip (seconds), feed it into a multi-speaker TTS model that was already trained across many voices. No extra training at inference time — fast, but similarity to the target voice is approximate.

Fine-tuning

Further train a pretrained TTS model's parameters on a target speaker's own recordings (minutes of audio, more compute and time). Produces a much closer voice and speaking-style match, at real training cost per speaker.

Zero-shot cloning means no training step at all for a new voice — just conditioning (e.g. YourTTS, VALL-E-style models). Few-shot means a light fine-tuning pass on a small amount of target-speaker data.

Voice conversion: a different approach

Voice cloning (above) is text-to-speech in a specific voice. Voice conversion (e.g. RVC — Retrieval-based Voice Conversion) is audio-to-audio instead: it takes existing audio — someone's singing or speech — and re-renders it in a target speaker's timbre while preserving the original content, pitch contour, and rhythm. No text or TTS model involved at all.

Ethical & consent considerations

This is the module most likely to be misused. Cloning a voice without consent enables impersonation, fraud (voice-phishing scam calls), and non-consensual deepfake audio. Any real project touching this should require explicit consent from the speaker being cloned, consider disclosure/watermarking of AI-generated audio, and be aware that the anti-spoofing techniques from lesson 07 exist specifically to defend against what this lesson makes possible.

A little history

  • 2018 — SV2TTSJia et al., "Transfer Learning from Speaker Verification to Multispeaker TTS," shows a speaker-verification embedding network can directly condition a TTS model on a new voice — the technique that underlies most modern voice cloning.
  • 2021 — YourTTSExtends the VITS architecture (lesson 09) to zero-shot, multilingual voice cloning from a short reference clip.
  • 2023 — VALL-EMicrosoft demonstrates high-fidelity zero-shot cloning from as little as 3 seconds of reference audio, treating voice cloning as a language-modeling problem over audio tokens.
  • 2023 — RVC popularizationRetrieval-based Voice Conversion becomes widely used in open-source communities, particularly for singing voice conversion.
  • 2023–ongoing — Regulatory responseAs cloning quality crosses into "convincing enough to scam or deceive," regulators begin responding directly — for example the US FCC's 2024 ruling classifying AI-voice robocalls under existing robocall law.

Evaluation

Speaker similarity — cosine similarity between a speaker-verification embedding (lesson 07) of the cloned output and of the real target voice — measures how close the clone actually sounds to the target. MOS (lesson 09) is still used alongside it to measure general naturalness, since a clone can be highly similar to the target speaker yet still sound robotic.
