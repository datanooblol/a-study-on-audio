08 · Speech Emotion Recognition

How something was said, not what was said.

Overview

Speech Emotion Recognition (SER) reads the paralinguistic signal in speech — pitch, energy, rhythm, voice quality — to infer the speaker's emotional state, independent of the words themselves. It closes out the "understand audio" arc: where ASR extracts what was said, SER (often paired with the transcript) extracts how. Common uses: call-center sentiment analytics, mental health monitoring, and adaptive voice assistants.

Key concepts

Categorical vs. dimensional emotion models

Categorical

Discrete labels — happy, sad, angry, neutral, fear, disgust, surprise — following Ekman's theory of basic emotions. Intuitive and easy to label, but forces messy, blended emotional states into one bucket.

Dimensional (valence-arousal)

Emotion as a point in continuous space: valence (negative ↔ positive) and arousal (calm ↔ excited), sometimes with a third dominance axis. More nuanced, but harder to annotate consistently.

Common datasets

CREMA-DCrowd-sourced actors across varied demographics reading the same lines with different emotions
A known limitation

Most benchmark datasets are acted (actors performing an emotion on cue), which doesn't fully match spontaneous real-world emotional speech. A model that does well on RAVDESS won't automatically generalize to a real, unscripted call-center recording — the acted-vs-spontaneous domain gap is a persistent open problem in this field.

Acoustic cues vs. learned embeddings

Classical SER hand-engineers acoustic correlates of emotion: pitch (F0) range and variance, energy/intensity, speaking rate, and voice-quality measures like jitter and shimmer (cycle-to-cycle instability in pitch/amplitude). The openSMILE toolkit standardizes these into well-known feature sets (e.g. eGeMAPS). As with every task in this curriculum, learned embeddings from self-supervised models (wav2vec2 fine-tuned for SER) now generally outperform hand-crafted features on their own.

Multimodal fusion

Emotion is carried by both how something is said (prosody) and what is said (word choice, sentiment). Combining an audio model with a text sentiment model (fusing predictions or features) generally beats either alone — though the two signals can also genuinely conflict, as with sarcasm, which remains a hard case for either modality on its own.

A little history

  • 1990s–2000s — Classical ML eraSVMs and GMMs trained on hand-crafted prosodic and spectral features become the standard approach.
  • 2008 — IEMOCAPReleased as one of the first large, multimodal, dyadic-interaction emotion datasets — still a major benchmark today.
  • 2010 — openSMILEStandardizes acoustic feature extraction for paralinguistics, used across the INTERSPEECH Computational Paralinguistics Challenge (ComParE) series.
  • Mid-2010s — Deep learning on spectrogramsCNNs and LSTMs trained directly on spectrograms start to outperform hand-crafted-feature + SVM pipelines.
  • Late 2010s–today — Self-supervised transfer learningFine-tuned wav2vec2/HuBERT models, often fused with text-sentiment models, become the state of the art.

Evaluation

For categorical models: accuracy and F1 per class, though because real data skews heavily toward "neutral," UAR (Unweighted Average Recall) — the average of per-class recall, ignoring class size — is the more common research metric, since it doesn't let a lazy "always predict neutral" model look artificially good.

For dimensional (valence-arousal) models: CCC (Concordance Correlation Coefficient) is the standard, measuring both correlation and agreement in scale between predicted and true continuous values.
